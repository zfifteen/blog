# The Problem With "Your Brain on ChatGPT"

A preprint from MIT's Media Lab is making the rounds with a striking claim: using ChatGPT to write essays measurably shrinks the brain's neural connectivity, accumulates "cognitive debt," and leaves students intellectually worse off than if they had never touched the tool. The paper, authored by Nataliya Kosmyna and colleagues and titled "Your Brain on ChatGPT," is ambitious in its scope. It combines EEG brain recordings, natural language processing analysis of student essays, and post-session interviews across 54 participants from five Boston-area universities. Over four months, participants wrote essays under one of three conditions: using only ChatGPT, using only a search engine, or using no external tools at all. The researchers measured brain activity during each session using a 32-electrode headset, computed directed connectivity between every pair of electrodes, and found that the "Brain-only" group consistently showed stronger neural coupling across every frequency band they analyzed. Their conclusion was that AI assistance reduces cognitive engagement in ways that matter for long-term learning. The paper has real ambition and some genuinely interesting behavioral findings. But its central claim rests on a structural flaw that the authors do not appear to recognize, and that flaw is serious enough to undercut the mechanistic story entirely.

The flaw is this: the three experimental groups were not doing the same task. They were doing three different tasks. The Brain-only group sat in front of a nearly blank document and typed continuously from memory. The LLM group alternated between composing prompts, reading ChatGPT's responses, evaluating the output, and occasionally copying or editing text. The Search Engine group browsed web pages, scrolled through content, clicked links across multiple tabs, and synthesized information from visually rich, information-dense pages. At the level of moment-to-moment physical behavior, these are radically different activities. They involve different patterns of eye movement, different motor outputs, different rhythms of attention, different visual stimulation, and different task-switching frequencies. The EEG headset sitting on each participant's head recorded all of it indiscriminately across the full 20-minute session, with no attempt to separate the neural signal of "thinking" from the neural signal of "scrolling" or "reading a chat interface." Every band-specific finding in the paper, whether it is the elevated alpha connectivity in Brain-only participants or the reduced theta coupling in the LLM group, is equally explainable by the sensorimotor differences between groups and by the "cognitive depth" hypothesis the authors prefer.

The authors interpret each EEG band through a well-established cognitive framework and do it fluently. Elevated alpha means internal semantic search and creative ideation. Elevated theta means higher working memory load. Delta dominance means large-scale integrative processing. These are legitimate interpretive frameworks when applied to controlled paradigms where the physical task is held constant across conditions. They are not legitimate here, because the physical task was never held constant. A Brain-only writer typing steadily for 20 minutes produces sustained motor cortex engagement, minimal visual complexity, and a continuous self-referential attention state, all of which would produce the exact EEG signature the authors label "cognitive depth." An LLM user pausing to read, then typing a short prompt, then waiting, then reading again produces a bursty, interrupted motor and attentional profile that would produce lower sustained connectivity in the same bands, for reasons that have nothing to do with whether they are thinking deeply or shallowly. The paper cites strong literature in support of each band interpretation, but the citations assume task equivalence that the design does not provide.

The behavioral findings are partially more defensible, but they carry their own unacknowledged problem. The quoting accuracy result is striking on the surface: in Session 1, zero out of 18 LLM participants could produce a correct quote from their own essay, while nearly all Search Engine and Brain-only participants could. But this result is almost entirely explained by a well-established phenomenon called the generation effect. Decades of memory research show that people remember content they produced themselves far better than content they passively received from an external source, regardless of how carefully they read or processed that content. The Brain-only group was asked to recall their own words. The LLM group was being asked to recall words that a language model wrote and they then incorporated. These are fundamentally different memory tasks dressed up as the same test. The comparison does not measure encoding depth. It measures source of content, and the design cannot separate the two.

None of this means the paper's directional intuition is wrong. There are plausible reasons to think that heavy reliance on AI for composition tasks changes how information is encoded, reduces the kind of generative struggle that deepens memory, and homogenizes written output in ways that matter educationally. The NLP findings showing greater essay similarity within the LLM group and the qualitative interview data showing reduced ownership are real signals worth taking seriously. The paper is also notable for its longitudinal design and the sheer volume of data it collects. But the specific causal and mechanistic claims, that LLM use measurably reduces neural connectivity in ways that indicate shallower cognitive engagement, cannot be supported by this design. The EEG findings are confounded by task-demand differences that the study never controls for and never acknowledges as a threat. The decisive test would be simple: record EEG from all three groups during a short, shared task, such as a five-minute free-writing-from-memory segment, at the start of each session, then compare connectivity during that matched baseline. If the group differences persist during identical behavior, the cognitive interpretation holds. If they vanish, the paper's most prominent finding is an artifact of different physical tasks producing different brain signals, which is not a finding about AI and cognition. It is a finding about what brains look like when they are doing different things, which is not news.
