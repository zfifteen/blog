# The Plasma Curve Is Lying to Us: What SARS-CoV-2 Antigen Data Actually Measures and Why It Is Breaking Long COVID Trials

When researchers publish a graph showing SARS-CoV-2 antigen levels in blood declining steadily over fourteen months after infection, the natural reading is reassuring. The virus is being cleared. The immune system is winning. Given enough time, the reservoir empties and the patient recovers. This interpretation feels correct because the curve looks exactly like textbook first-order viral clearance, the kind of smooth exponential decay that appears in every immunology course when describing how the body eliminates a pathogen. The shape is familiar, the slope is consistent, and nothing about the graph signals that anything unusual is happening. A recent study published in The Lancet Infectious Diseases by Peluso and colleagues documented precisely this pattern in a well-characterized cohort of 171 adults followed for fourteen months after RNA-confirmed SARS-CoV-2 infection. Antigen prevalence was elevated compared to pre-pandemic controls at every timepoint, then gradually declined toward background levels. The authors concluded that their data provide strong evidence that SARS-CoV-2 persists in some form for up to fourteen months following acute infection. That conclusion is probably correct. But the interpretation attached to the declining curve, that persistence is gradually resolving as the reservoir shrinks, is almost certainly wrong, and the error has consequences that extend far beyond one study.

The problem begins with what a plasma antigen assay actually measures. It does not measure how much virus is present in tissue. It measures how much viral protein successfully travels from wherever the virus is hiding, through the intervening biology of the human body, into a blood sample drawn from a peripheral vein. That journey is not passive. Between a gut reservoir and a blood draw sits the liver, one of the most immunologically active organs in the body, densely populated with Kupffer cells whose primary function is to capture and destroy opsonized particles arriving through the portal circulation. Any antigen produced by virus replicating in the gut must pass through this hepatic checkpoint before reaching the systemic circulation where the assay can detect it. In the weeks immediately after acute infection, antibody titers are low and antibody affinity is modest, so antigen slips through this checkpoint relatively easily. Over the following months, something changes. Not the reservoir. The checkpoint. Antibody affinity matures through ongoing germinal center activity, IgG molecules progressively bind antigen with higher efficiency, and Kupffer cell capture becomes increasingly complete. The plasma curve falls not because the gut is clearing but because the liver is getting better at intercepting what the gut produces. The reservoir and the assay are measuring different things, and the curve reflects only one of them.


This distinction would be merely academic if there were no independent way to assess reservoir status. But there is. The Peluso PET imaging study, examining gut tissue directly rather than inferring it from blood, found that all five biopsies taken at timepoints where plasma antigen was approaching the detection threshold showed active SARS-CoV-2 replication intermediates. Not just antigen fragments. Replication intermediates, which require active ongoing viral transcription and cannot be explained by dead virus or protein debris. The gut was running at full capacity at the exact moment the plasma curve suggested the reservoir was nearly empty. This is not a marginal discrepancy that falls within measurement error. It is a categorical contradiction. Under the conventional interpretation of the plasma curve, near-zero plasma antigen means near-zero viral burden. The tissue data say the opposite. Either the plasma curve is tracking something other than reservoir size, or five consecutive biopsies are all false positives. The hepatic filtration argument explains the contradiction cleanly. The tissue data break the illusion that the decay curve creates.


The implications compound when you consider which patients go plasma-negative first. If the mechanism driving plasma clearance is antibody-mediated hepatic gating rather than actual viral clearance, then stronger immune responders, patients who develop higher-affinity antibodies faster, will reach plasma negativity earlier and more completely. This sounds like good news until you follow the logic to its conclusion. Those same patients may harbor tissue reservoirs of equivalent size to weaker immune responders, but their more efficient hepatic filtration system is intercepting a greater fraction of the antigen before it reaches peripheral blood. Immune success masks reservoir persistence. The patients most likely to be told by a plasma assay that they are clear are disproportionately the ones with the strongest antibody responses, not necessarily the smallest reservoirs. Any study that correlates plasma antigen status with Long COVID symptoms will systematically misclassify this group, placing them in the negative category regardless of their tissue burden. The association between viral persistence and chronic symptoms will be underestimated in exactly the patients whose immune systems appear most functional.


This matters enormously for clinical trials, but to understand why, it is necessary to recognize a second error that runs parallel to the filtration problem. Not all SARS-CoV-2 antigens detected in plasma after acute infection are evidence of the same biological process. Spike protein sits on the outer surface of the virus and can be captured by immune cells in lymph nodes, displayed on the surface of follicular dendritic cells, and maintained as an immunological archive for months after the virus itself has been cleared. It requires no ongoing viral replication. Nucleocapsid protein, by contrast, is locked inside the viral particle and only appears in blood when cells are actively disrupted and intact viral debris is released into the circulation. A plasma sample with detectable spike but no nucleocapsid is therefore consistent with a patient whose virus is long gone and whose immune system is recirculating archived antigen fragments in lymph node germinal centers. A plasma sample with both spike and nucleocapsid in roughly equal proportions is consistent with ongoing active replication producing intact viral particles that are lysing cells and spilling their contents into the bloodstream. These are not two measurements of the same phenomenon. They are measurements of two entirely different biological states that happen to share the same diagnostic label.


The field has not made this distinction because every study to date has treated spike, nucleocapsid, and S1 as interchangeable signals for the same underlying fact, that virus is present somewhere. The error is understandable in its origins. During acute infection, when viral burden is high and compartmentalization is less important, all three antigens track together and all reliably indicate active replication. That interpretive equivalence was carried into post-acute research without re-examination, even though the biological context had changed completely. At low levels of viral persistence in sequestered compartments, the source, stability, and transport pathway of each antigen diverges sharply. The ratio between spike and nucleocapsid in plasma becomes a topographic signal encoding not just whether virus is present but where it is and what it is doing. A high spike-to-nucleocapsid ratio points toward lymph node antigen archiving. A ratio near one-to-one points toward active replication with cellular lysis. These are not redundant checks. They are a map of reservoir biology, and every study that pooled them into a single positivity score discarded that map without knowing it existed.


The consequences for antiviral trials are severe and specific. Consider a patient enrolled in a Long COVID antiviral trial on the basis of plasma antigen positivity. The patient has a spike-to-nucleocapsid ratio of ten to one, indicating lymph node antigen archiving rather than active replication. The trial administers an antiviral drug that works perfectly, completely suppressing whatever residual viral replication might be occurring. Nucleocapsid production stops. But plasma spike is being sustained by follicular dendritic cell display in lymph nodes, a process that has nothing to do with viral replication and cannot be affected by an antiviral drug. The plasma spike level does not move. The trial records no change in its primary endpoint and concludes the drug is ineffective. The drug is declared to have failed. The field concludes that viral persistence is not driving Long COVID symptoms in this population. None of those conclusions follow from what actually happened, which is that the trial enrolled the wrong patients, measured the wrong endpoint, and was structurally incapable of detecting the treatment effect it was designed to find. The assay did not fail. The trial design failed by not understanding what the assay measures.


The population-level consequence of this design failure is a specific instance of a well-known statistical trap. When a trial enrolls patients who cannot respond to the intervention and mixes them with patients who can, and then averages the results across both groups, the responder signal is diluted by the non-responder majority. If the non-responding group is large enough, the true treatment effect disappears into noise entirely. Given the Peluso data showing spike as the dominant signal in roughly 5% of post-COVID patients while nucleocapsid was detected in only 2.3%, the spike-dominant, non-responding population likely outnumbers the nucleocapsid-positive, potentially responding population by a substantial margin. Any trial that enrolled on antigen positivity without stratifying by ratio would have built this dilution into its design from the first patient enrolled. This is not a failure of statistical power that more patients can solve. It is a failure of biological stratification that requires a fundamentally different enrollment criterion. The patients who need to be in these trials are not everyone with any detectable plasma antigen. They are the subset with nucleocapsid positivity or a ratio close to one-to-one, and that subset has probably never been cleanly enrolled in any trial yet conducted.


The corrective path is more tractable than the problem might suggest, because the necessary data already exist. The Simoa platform used by Peluso and by most other groups running post-COVID antigen studies measures spike, S1, and nucleocapsid simultaneously in the same sample. Every study that ran a multiplex panel collected the information needed to compute spike-to-nucleocapsid ratios at every timepoint. That calculation was never performed because the question was never asked. Reanalyzing existing trial datasets to stratify outcomes by pre-treatment ratio requires no new patients, no new technology, and no new samples. It requires only recognizing that two columns of existing data encode a biological distinction that changes the interpretation of every result in the dataset. The prediction is specific and falsifiable: patients with high spike-to-nucleocapsid ratios at enrollment should show no antiviral-driven plasma clearance, while patients with ratios near one-to-one should be the subgroup where plasma antigen tracks treatment response. If that split does not emerge from the data, the stratification hypothesis fails. If it does emerge, it retroactively explains the inconsistent results that have accumulated across multiple trials and points directly toward what the next generation of trials must do differently.


The argument developed here leads to a conclusion that is uncomfortable for a field that has invested heavily in plasma antigen monitoring as its primary window into viral persistence. Long COVID is probably not one disease. It is at minimum two diseases with a shared acute trigger and a shared name, diverging at the point where the immune system either archives viral antigen in germinal centers or sustains active replication in tissue reservoirs. Patients in the first group have an immunological problem, specifically the persistence of archived antigen driving ongoing immune activation, and they need interventions targeting germinal center antigen turnover, not viral replication. Patients in the second group have a virological problem and may benefit from antivirals, but only if those drugs are tested in trials that enroll them specifically and measure endpoints that reflect replication status rather than archived antigen levels. Every trial that has enrolled mixed populations and measured plasma antigen clearance as a primary endpoint has been averaging these two groups together and calling the combined null result evidence that antiviral treatment does not work. The plasma curve looks like clearance. The tissue data say otherwise. The ratio encodes the difference. Until trials are designed around that distinction, the field will continue mistaking a filtration artifact for biology and a design failure for a treatment failure.
